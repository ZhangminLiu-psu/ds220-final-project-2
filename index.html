Who Gets to Speak on Screen?¶

A 44-Year Look at the Bechdel Test in Hollywood Films¶

DS 220, Project II  |  Min Liu  |  Spring 2026

The Bechdel Test asks whether a film has two named women who talk to each other about something other than a man. I use it as a lens on 1,794 films from 1970 to 2013.

1. Problem Space¶

The Bechdel Test is a three-question check on whether a film treats women as full characters. I am asking whether Hollywood has gotten better at this over time, and whether doing so is good or bad for business. Audiences, executives, filmmakers, and advocacy groups all care for different reasons.

The data covers US theatrical releases from 1970 to 2013, with results from bechdeltest.com and financials from The Numbers, aggregated by FiveThirtyEight.

2. Questions to Address¶

  1. Has the pass rate risen, fallen, or stayed flat?
  2. Which decade was best, which was worst?
  3. Do bigger-budget films pass less often?
  4. Do passing films earn more, less, or the same on a per-budget basis?
  5. Is the ROI distribution shape different for passing and failing films?
  6. What is the most common failure mode?
  7. Is the "two named women" floor shrinking?
  8. Do representation and money move together over time?

3. The Dataset¶

Source: FiveThirtyEight's Bechdel Test dataset, released with Walt Hickey's 2014 article "The Dollar-and-Cents Case Against Hollywood's Exclusion of Women." Each row is one film.

Column Meaning
year, title, imdb Release year, title, IMDb id
clean_test ok, dubious, men, notalk, or nowomen
binary PASS or FAIL
budget_2013$, domgross_2013$, intgross_2013$ Inflation-adjusted to 2013 USD

I use the inflation-adjusted columns throughout.

Cleaning¶

The gross columns load as strings due to '#N/A' markers, so I coerce to numeric. I also fix &amp; in titles and add decade and worldgross_2013$ columns for later.

In [6]:
for col in ['domgross', 'intgross', 'domgross_2013$', 'intgross_2013$']:
    df[col] = pd.to_numeric(df[col], errors='coerce')

df['title'] = df['title'].str.replace('&amp;', '&', regex=False)
df['decade'] = (df['year'] // 10) * 10

df['worldgross_2013$'] = df['domgross_2013$'].fillna(0) + df['intgross_2013$'].fillna(0)
df['roi'] = (df['worldgross_2013$'] - df['budget_2013$']) / df['budget_2013$']

15. Insights¶

  • Pass rate has plateaued near 55% since about 1995.
  • Passing and failing films return the same multiple of their budget, so the financial argument against representation does not survive the data.
  • The most common failure is "women who never talk to each other," not "no women."
  • The "two named women" floor is the clearest improving metric, hitting 92% by the 2010s.
  • The biggest gap between passing and failing films is in budget, not box office.

Limitations. Bechdel is coarse, the data ends in 2013, the 1970s sample is sparse, and nothing here is causal.

16. Conclusion¶

Pass rate has improved slowly across 44 years, and the "audiences vote against it" claim is not borne out in the data. More than half of failing films already have two or more named women cast, the women just do not talk to each other, so this is a writers'-room problem, not a casting one.
